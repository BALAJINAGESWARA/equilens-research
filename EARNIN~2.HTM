Revenue & Earnings Growth Analysis

What drove the top line: AI semiconductor revenue nearly doubled sequentially, from $8.4B in FQ1 to $10.8B in FQ2, powered by escalating orders for custom AI accelerators (XPUs) from hyperscaler customers — including at least two known large cloud providers — and AI networking ASICs. This segment alone accounted for ~49% of total company revenue, up from ~44% last quarter. Non-AI semiconductor revenue ($4.2B, +6% YoY) showed tentative recovery in broadband, industrial, and enterprise networking, though remains well below peak cycle levels.

Infrastructure software grew a solid 9% YoY to $7.18B, reflecting VMware subscription migration progress (ARR +17%) and cross-selling of VCF (VMware Cloud Foundation). The miss vs. whisper expectations reflects a tougher comparison and slower-than-anticipated enterprise deal closures — not structural deterioration, but it dented sentiment given the high bar set by AI.

What drove EPS: Operating leverage was the headline — the 69% EBITDA margin (vs. 68% in FQ1) reflects AI semiconductor gross margins (~70%) scaling while software margins hold near 93%. The EPS beat vs. consensus also benefited from continued share repurchases. GAAP vs. non-GAAP gap ($1.91 vs. $2.44) reflects ongoing amortization of VMware acquisition intangibles (~$3.3B this quarter, annualizing $13B+).

Industry & Demand Analysis

TAM & cycle position: The AI ASIC/networking market is estimated to reach $150–200B+ by 2027–2028 (per multiple sell-side estimates), growing at 40–60% CAGR. Broadcom, with its custom XPU/ASIC platform, sits at an inflection point: hyperscaler AI capex is accelerating, not plateauing. Per the company's own guidance, Q3 AI semis of $16B implies run-rate AI revenue of ~$64B annualized — already ahead of the $56B FY2026 target by Q3.

AI infrastructure spend: Google, Meta, Microsoft, and Amazon have all publicly committed to multi-hundred-billion dollar AI infrastructure capex cycles over 2025–2027. Each has or is developing custom AI silicon partnerships — Broadcom's ASIC design and packaging expertise positions it as a primary beneficiary. AI networking (Jericho/Tomahawk) is a high-margin adjacency benefiting from the scale-out of AI clusters.

Non-AI semiconductor cycle: Recovery is tentative. Broadband and enterprise networking (+6% YoY in non-AI semis) are normalizing post-inventory correction, but below peak. Industrial and storage remain soft. The next catalyst for non-AI semis is an enterprise refresh cycle — likely late 2026.

Competitive landscape: NVIDIA dominates merchant GPU; Broadcom differentiates by offering custom silicon with better energy efficiency for specific workloads. Marvell Technology is the closest ASIC competitor (custom NPUs for Microsoft Azure, Amazon Trainium), but trails Broadcom in design win volume and hyperscaler penetration depth. Intel and AMD have limited custom ASIC exposure at scale. The market is effectively a two-player race (Broadcom + Marvell) for custom AI silicon, a structural advantage.

🐻 Bear Case

  • AI capex pullback: If hyperscaler ROI scrutiny intensifies or macro deteriorates, AI infrastructure capex could stall faster than consensus expects. Broadcom has little revenue diversification outside these customers for AI.

  • Software deceleration: If VMware migration stalls or enterprise IT spend tightens further, the 9% YoY software growth could decelerate — removing one of the two legs supporting the earnings story.
  • NVIDIA risk to networking: NVIDIA's Spectrum-X networking platform competes directly with Broadcom's Tomahawk/Jericho AI networking franchise. NVIDIA's integrated GPU+NIC+switch ecosystem could gradually displace Broadcom's networking ASICs in GPU clusters.

Long-Term (Structural)

High

Hyperscaler in-sourcing of ASIC

Google's TPUv5, Amazon's Trainium 2, and Microsoft's Maia 100 all represent efforts to reduce dependence on third-party AI silicon. Accelerating in-house design capability could shrink Broadcom's addressable share over a 3–5 year horizon.

NVIDIA networking displacement

NVIDIA's Spectrum-X ecosystem integrates GPU, NIC, and switch functions, potentially making standalone AI networking ASICs (Broadcom's Tomahawk/Jericho) redundant for GPU-centric clusters. This is a structural threat to a high-margin business unit.

Medium

Geopolitical / export controls

U.S. chip export restrictions to China could limit Broadcom's non-AI semiconductor revenue in networking and broadband end-markets. Any expansion of AI chip controls to allied-country partners would also create compliance complexity.

Investment Thesis & Verdict

The core case for owning AVGO: Broadcom is one of the few companies in the world with the engineering depth to design competitive custom AI ASICs at hyperscaler scale, combined with a 93% gross margin software business compounding in the background. The AI revenue trajectory — $10.8B this quarter, $16.0B guided next quarter, $56B for the full year, $100B targeted for FY2027 — is among the most visible large-cap revenue ramps in technology history, supported by multi-year contractual commitments. FCF of $10.26B in a single quarter funds aggressive capital return and leaves room for continued R&D investment. The post-AH pullback of ~14% on what is objectively a strong quarter (record revenue, record EBITDA, above-consensus Q3 guide) is a whisper-number overreaction, not a fundamental deterioration.

What would change the thesis: A reduction in hyperscaler AI capex commitments, confirmed acceleration of in-house ASIC development by Google or Meta, two-or-more consecutive software misses that call VMware ARR growth into question, or macro-driven multiple compression across the broader semiconductor complex. None of these are imminent, but all are real risks worth monitoring each quarter.
